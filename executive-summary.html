Why Integrated Fire Management

Fewer fire hotspots do not mean lower risk

Across Latin America's forests, savannas and the Amazon, wildfire is no longer a seasonal nuisance but a structural threat — driven by forest degradation, agricultural expansion and a changing climate. Counting active fires hides the deeper drivers; reducing them requires prevention, not only suppression.

Yet fire is also part of how many landscapes and peoples live. Indigenous Peoples and local communities have used fire safely for generations, and cultural fire use — when recognised and integrated — can reduce fuel loads and prevent the catastrophic fires that destroy ecosystems and livelihoods.

Integrated Fire Management (IFM) brings these truths together: it balances prevention, preparedness, response and recovery; it works with communities rather than against them; and it treats fire as a phenomenon to be governed across borders, sectors and knowledge systems.

FAO launched the Global Fire Management Hub in 2023 with UNEP and partners to help countries make this shift. With Canada's support, this project turned that vision into concrete capacity, tools, policy and partnerships on the ground.

"The challenge is not the absence of fire, but the absence of integrated, anticipatory management that puts communities and ecosystems first."

— Framing of the Global Fire Management Hub

Objective & structure

One global response, built on five strategic pillars

The project worked toward a single overarching goal — improved conservation and restoration of ecosystems through IFM in partner countries — organised around the Fire Hub's five strategic, cross-cutting pillars.

1

Knowledge & data sharing

A one-stop digital platform, a regional cooperation map, and gender-sensitive knowledge products and outreach.

2

IFM policy development & implementation

Interoperability for fire emergencies and stronger regional and international fire-management networks.

3

Capacity building

Gender-responsive, targeted IFM training at national and regional levels, anchored in real capacity-gap assessments.

4

Fire risk reduction & early warning

Better fire data, monitoring, and early-warning tools — including a fire module for multi-hazard early-warning systems.

5

Wildfire-resilient communities

Indigenous Peoples and local communities engaged as co-managers of fire, with knowledge, technologies and recognition.

Where the project worked

Global in mandate with a Latin America & Caribbean focus, the project engaged Bolivia, Peru, Colombia and Ecuador directly, supported the Pan-Amazon region (with Brazil/IPAM), and strengthened regional cooperation across the LAC fire-management community — connecting to global mechanisms such as the Global Fire Monitoring Centre and the international Community-Based Fire Management network.

Pillar 1 · Immediate Outcome 1110Knowledge & data sharing

The Global Fire Management Hub digital platform was developed as a 'one-stop digital shop on IFM', transferring existing materials — including resources from the Global Fire Monitoring Centre (GFMC) — and curating, rather than duplicating, the best available knowledge, tools and guidance so users can find what they need in one place.

To make the regional landscape legible, the project produced a comprehensive cooperation map of IFM initiatives in Latin America and the Caribbean — research and compilation across organisations and networks — laying the basis for a dynamic decision-support tool that improves visibility, coordination and strategic alignment among a fast-growing community of actors.

A suite of gender-sensitive knowledge products and communications — booklets, a fire glossary, a self-assessment tool and multimedia outreach (including a fire mascot engaging women, men, girls and boys) — carried IFM concepts to diverse audiences.

Pillar 1 · in the field

Turning knowledge into shared understanding

Knowledge products only matter when they reach people. The project paired its digital platform and cooperation map with face-to-face exchange — working sessions, posters and locally-adapted materials — so that fire managers, technicians and community leaders built a common language for IFM. A recurring lesson, now shaping delivery: technical information must be translated into accessible, territorially relevant formats to be used.

The one-stop platform was designed to curate and connect users to high-quality existing resources — including from the Global Fire Monitoring Centre — rather than duplicate content, keeping it accessible and easy to navigate. The accompanying cooperation map made the regional landscape legible, charting 43 initiatives across projects, programmes and networks to improve visibility, coordination and strategic alignment among a fast-growing community of actors.

2
Pillar 2 · Immediate Outcome 1120IFM policy development & cooperation

Fire is transboundary, and the project treated it that way. It supported the design of an interoperability mechanism for fire emergencies — compiling data and drafting cooperation activities, guidelines and standard operating procedures to help countries act together when fire crosses borders.

It strengthened regional and international fire-management networks, convening Latin American fire experts and networks (such as the regional fire-expert groups and the Amazon Integrated Fire Management network) to develop a regional action plan, and supporting expert engagement at the Fire Hub Plenary (June 2025) and related global fora.

At country level, diagnostics in Bolivia, Peru, Colombia and Ecuador analysed legal and institutional frameworks — surfacing a shared finding: moving from the prohibition of fire toward its responsible regulation is the enabling condition for IFM.

Pillar 3 · Immediate Outcome 1210Capacity building & training

Capacity is the backbone of IFM. Beginning with a regional assessment of countries' capacity gaps, needs and priorities, the project developed and delivered regional training — building on and aligning with existing programmes and networks, and partnering with institutions that have a permanent presence in the territory.

The results exceeded expectations: 339 national and regional actors participated in IFM trainings against a target of 100, and a further 191 in dedicated fire-data training against a target of 30 — with deliberate attention to women's participation.

A key lesson now shaping delivery: build capacity progressively in the same territory — from basic to advanced — and site specialised courses at inter-regional 'nodes' to make the most of scarce qualified trainers.

FAO · Global Fire Management Hub12

Pillar 3 · in the field

Standardising skills, certifying responders

Country diagnostics pointed to a clear recommendation the project is helping advance: standardise forest-firefighter training and certify instructors, so that responders across institutions and borders 'speak the same technical language'. Peru's standardised curriculum for forest-fire combatants is a positive precedent the region can build on.

Two further lessons now shape how training is delivered. First, build capacity progressively in the same territory — from basic to advanced — rather than through one-off courses, and site specialised training at inter-regional 'nodes' to make the most of scarce qualified trainers. Second, anchor training to institutions with a permanent presence in the territory, so follow-up and continuity outlast the project. The regional package was developed to build on and align with existing programmes, including the Latin American Model Forest Network.

4
Pillar 4 · Immediate Outcome 1220Fire data, early warning & monitoring

Good decisions need good data. The project improved the understanding and use of fire data through regional training, and conducted a regional stocktake of Fire Danger Rating Systems (FDRS) and Early Warning Systems (EWS) across Latin America and the Caribbean — assessing their operational maturity and their ability to translate data into timely, coordinated action.

It also advanced a fire module within multi-hazard early-warning systems (MHEWS), aligned with the global 'Early Warnings for All' agenda, and prototyped decision-aids that integrate science with traditional knowledge.

The guiding lesson: an alert that does not reach the community in an actionable format prevents no fire — technical metrics must become culturally appropriate guidance, delivered through the channels people actually use.

Evidence for action

Together these products move the region from counting fires toward understanding risk — the foundation for prevention, anticipatory action and the structural investments that lower vulnerability over time.

Pillar 5 · Immediate Outcome 1310Indigenous Peoples & resilient communities

Indigenous Peoples and local communities are the first to face fire — and often the most effective at preventing it. The project facilitated intercultural workshops centred on communities, fostering exchange of traditional knowledge, cultural perspectives and technical expertise, and undertook gender-sensitive consultations with rural women's organisations and community leaders.

It is developing a knowledge product on the key role of Indigenous Peoples and local knowledge holders in IFM (a Community-Based Fire Management review), and supported demonstration activities through regional initiatives such as the Latin American Model Forest Network.

The engagement far exceeded targets: 508 Indigenous and local knowledge holders engaged (target 100) and 588 representatives gained access to IFM knowledge and technologies (target 100) — with gender responsiveness treated not as an add-on but as an operational necessity.

FAO · Global Fire Management Hub15

Pillar 5 · in the field

Fire as a tool, governed with communities

Where cultural fire use is recognised and integrated, fire becomes a managed tool rather than a threat — reducing fuel loads, protecting food security and preventing catastrophic fire. The project documented and helped integrate ancestral fire knowledge into management plans, and worked to clarify the role of community brigades beyond 'combat' — in prevention, monitoring and responsible fire use.

Concrete examples show the approach in action: in Bolivia's protected areas, Ayoreo and Chiquitano fire knowledge — hunting practices and the traditional chaqueo — is being codified into management plans, paired with safe-burning techniques and firebreaks; and through technical dialogue, communities have adapted traditional planting calendars to a changing climate using weather-station data. Gender-responsive consultations with rural women's organisations ensured these processes amplified, rather than overlooked, women's voices.

12 · Lessons & recommendationsFAO Global Fire Management Hub · GCP/GLO/1351/CAN

For the donor & the next phase

What we learned — and what it implies

Distilled from the project's diagnostics, roadmaps, workshops and the IFM paper, these are the lessons most relevant to sustaining and scaling the investment.

From prohibition to regulation

Legal recognition of responsible, prescribed fire is the enabling condition for IFM. Reforming punitive frameworks unlocks community-based prevention.

Put communities at the centre

Indigenous Peoples and local communities are the first responders; their centrality, recognition and direct access to resources are preconditions for results.

Make alerts actionable

Technical excellence is wasted if the alert does not reach communities in a usable format. Invest in the 'last mile' of early warning.

Build capacity progressively

Consolidate training in the same territory from basic to advanced, anchored in institutions with permanent local presence.

Act regionally on a transboundary problem

Fire crosses borders; interoperable systems, harmonised protocols and a standing Pan-Amazon mechanism are needed.

Fund prevention, structurally

Fewer hotspots do not mean lower risk. Sustained budget lines for prevention, equipment and coordination are essential.

Standardise & certify training

A shared curriculum and instructor certification let responders coordinate safely across institutions and borders.

Connect science to decision-making

A standing science–policy interface ensures research and monitoring actually reach the people who make fire-management decisions.

The full set of 33 lessons, best practices, findings and technical recommendations — each linked to its source deliverable — is available in the FireHub Learnings register, organised into seven themes:

Policy & governance Indigenous Peoples & traditional knowledge Capacity building & training Fire data & early warning Regional cooperation Community fire management Knowledge & communication

For a next phase, these point to a clear agenda: anchor IFM in enabling law, resource prevention structurally, invest in the 'last mile' of early warning, and keep Indigenous Peoples and local communities at the centre — sustained by dedicated budget lines and a standing regional coordination mechanism.

Distilled from the country diagnostics and roadmaps (Bolivia, Peru, Ecuador), the binational and Pan-Amazon workshop reports, the Integrated Fire Management paper, the LAC early-warning mapping and the IFM assessment tool.

The road ahead

A foundation built to last

Canada's contribution did more than deliver outputs — it catalysed further resource mobilisation, enabling additional FAO-led projects to continue the Fire Hub's work. The platform, cooperation map, maturity assessment, training package and knowledge products are designed as living tools that countries and partners will keep using and adapting.

What continues: final validation and rollout of selected tools; official launch of the regional IFM course; nationwide dissemination of communication materials; the standing Pan-Amazon coordination mechanism; and the postgraduate IFM track advancing with regional institutions.

What it needs: the project's own evidence is clear — durable impact depends on dedicated budget lines, institutionalised mechanisms, and continued investment in the 'last mile' of training, equipment and early warning that reaches communities.
